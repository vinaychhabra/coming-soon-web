01 Walk me through the main Google Ads campaign types and when you would use each one. CORE ▼
Model Answer
Google Ads offers several campaign types, each suited to different business goals:

Search Campaigns — Text ads on Google Search results. Best for capturing high-intent buyers who are already searching for a product or service. Ideal for direct response and lead generation.

Display Campaigns — Visual banner ads across the Google Display Network (millions of sites and apps). Best for brand awareness, retargeting, and reaching users earlier in the funnel.

Shopping Campaigns — Product listing ads showing images, price, and store name. Essential for e-commerce businesses wanting to drive product sales.

Video Campaigns (YouTube) — In-stream and discovery ads on YouTube. Best for storytelling, brand building, or reaching audiences by interest and demographics.

Performance Max (PMax) — Google's AI-driven campaign that runs across all channels (Search, Display, YouTube, Gmail, Maps) simultaneously, using machine learning to optimize toward a conversion goal. Best when you have a clear conversion objective and sufficient data.

App Campaigns — For mobile app installs and in-app actions across Google's entire network.
When asked this at TP, tie each type to a client use case — e.g. "A local restaurant would start with Search and Maps; an e-commerce client would layer Shopping + PMax."
02 How does the Google Ads auction work? What determines where your ad appears? CORE ▼
Model Answer
Every time someone searches on Google, an auction runs instantly. Your ad's position is determined by Ad Rank, which is calculated as:

Ad Rank = Bid × Quality Score × Expected Impact of Extensions

Quality Score (1–10) is made up of three components:
  • Expected Click-Through Rate (CTR) — how likely users are to click your ad
  • Ad Relevance — how closely the ad matches the search intent
  • Landing Page Experience — how relevant and fast the destination page is
This means a higher bid alone doesn't guarantee top position. A highly relevant ad with a great landing page can outrank a competitor with a higher bid. This is why optimizing Quality Score is central to reducing cost-per-click and improving ROI.
This is foundational — expect this in almost every Google Ads interview. Practice explaining it as if teaching it to a client who has never advertised before.
03 What are match types in Google Ads and how do they affect campaign performance? CORE ▼
Model Answer
Match types control which searches trigger your ads:
  • Broad Match — Widest reach; Google's AI matches to related searches, including synonyms and related topics. Highest volume but can attract irrelevant traffic. Works best with Smart Bidding.
  • Phrase Match — Ads show for searches containing the meaning of your keyword. More targeted than broad but more flexible than exact. Good balance of reach and relevance.
  • Exact Match — Ads only show for searches with the same intent as the exact keyword. Highest precision, lowest volume, typically highest CTR and conversion rate.
Negative keywords are equally important — they prevent ads from showing for irrelevant queries, protecting budget and improving Quality Score.

A strong campaign usually uses a layered match type strategy: Exact for best-performing terms, Phrase for discovery, and Broad with Smart Bidding for scale — while maintaining a robust negative keyword list.
04 Explain the difference between CPC, CPM, CPA, and ROAS bidding strategies. CORE ▼
Model Answer
CPC (Cost Per Click) – Manual: You set the maximum you'll pay per click. Best for hands-on control when you're still learning what works. Good for new campaigns with limited data.

CPM (Cost Per Thousand Impressions): Pay per 1,000 views. Used in Display and YouTube campaigns focused on brand awareness where clicks aren't the goal.

CPA (Cost Per Acquisition / Target CPA): Google's Smart Bidding sets bids automatically to get conversions at or below your target cost. Requires at least 30–50 conversions/month to work effectively. Best for lead generation.

ROAS (Return on Ad Spend / Target ROAS): Google optimizes for revenue relative to spend. E.g., a 400% ROAS target means you want $4 back for every $1 spent. Requires robust conversion value tracking. Ideal for e-commerce.

Maximize Conversions / Maximize Clicks: Fully automated — Google spends the budget to get the most conversions or clicks. Good for accounts with consistent budgets and clear goals.

The right strategy depends on the campaign's stage and data maturity — early on, manual CPC; as data builds, move toward Smart Bidding.
05 What is a conversion in Google Ads and how is conversion tracking set up? CORE ▼
Model Answer
A conversion is a measurable action that a user takes after clicking an ad, aligned with a business goal. Examples include:
  • Form submission or lead inquiry
  • Purchase or checkout completion
  • Phone call from the ad or website
  • App download or in-app action
  • Newsletter signup or account creation
Setting up conversion tracking involves:
  1. Going to Tools → Conversions in Google Ads
  2. Defining the conversion action and its value (if applicable)
  3. Installing the Google Ads conversion tag on the thank-you/confirmation page via Google Tag Manager or direct code
  4. Verifying the tag is firing correctly
For new clients without a developer, Google Tag Manager is the recommended implementation path — it lets you deploy tags without touching code directly.

Without conversion tracking, Smart Bidding cannot optimize effectively, and you cannot measure ROI. This is one of the first things I'd help a new client set up.
At TP, new advertiser onboarding often starts with conversion setup. Frame this as a priority in your answer.
06 What is Quality Score, why does it matter, and how would you improve it? CORE ▼
Model Answer
Quality Score is Google's 1–10 rating of the relevance and quality of your keywords, ads, and landing pages. It directly impacts:
  • Ad Rank — higher QS can place you above higher-bidding competitors
  • Cost Per Click — higher QS means you pay less per click for the same position
To improve Quality Score:

1. CTR: Write compelling, relevant ad copy. Use numbers, offers, and strong CTAs. A/B test headlines regularly using Responsive Search Ads.

2. Ad Relevance: Tightly theme your ad groups — each should focus on one topic with keywords that closely match the ad copy. Avoid dumping dozens of unrelated keywords into one ad group.

3. Landing Page Experience: Ensure the landing page directly matches what the ad promises. Fast load time (Core Web Vitals), mobile-friendly design, clear CTA, and relevant content all matter. Use Google's PageSpeed Insights tool to diagnose issues.
07 What is remarketing in Google Ads and how would you use it for a new advertiser? CORE ▼
Model Answer
Remarketing (also called retargeting) lets you show ads specifically to people who have previously visited your website or interacted with your brand. It works by placing a cookie or tag on site visitors and then targeting those users through Google Display Network or Search campaigns.

Types of remarketing:
  • Standard Remarketing — show display ads to past website visitors
  • Dynamic Remarketing — show ads featuring the specific products/services a user viewed (powerful for e-commerce)
  • RLSA (Remarketing Lists for Search Ads) — adjust Search bids or show different ads for users who've previously visited your site
  • Customer Match — upload a customer email list to target existing customers or create similar audiences
For a new advertiser: I'd start by installing the remarketing tag (via Google Tag Manager) and building a "website visitors in last 30 days" audience. Even if the audience is initially small, it allows us to test messaging and build conversion data before scaling. As traffic grows, we can create segmented lists — cart abandoners, product page viewers, etc. — for more personalized campaigns.
08 What are Responsive Search Ads (RSAs) and how do they differ from older Expanded Text Ads? CORE ▼
Model Answer
Responsive Search Ads (RSAs) are the current standard ad format in Google Search campaigns. You provide up to 15 headlines and 4 description lines, and Google's machine learning tests different combinations to find what performs best for each user's search query.

Key differences from Expanded Text Ads (ETAs):
  • ETAs had a fixed structure: 3 headlines + 2 descriptions. You controlled the exact combination shown.
  • RSAs are flexible and adaptive — Google dynamically assembles the best combination for each auction.
  • ETAs were sunset by Google in 2022; RSAs are now the only editable text ad format.
Best practices for RSAs:
  • Write at least 8–10 truly distinct headlines (avoid repetition)
  • Include keywords in at least 2–3 headlines
  • Include a strong CTA in at least one headline and one description
  • Use "Pin" sparingly — only when a headline MUST appear in a specific position
  • Aim for an "Excellent" Ad Strength rating
09 What are ad extensions (now called "assets") and why are they important? CORE ▼
Model Answer
Ad Assets (formerly Extensions) add extra information to your ads, making them larger, more useful, and more clickable — without extra cost per click. They improve Ad Rank because Google rewards ads that provide a better user experience.

Key asset types and their uses:
  • Sitelink Assets — Additional links below the ad (e.g., "Book a Demo," "View Pricing," "Contact Us") — great for directing users to specific pages
  • Callout Assets — Short, non-clickable highlights (e.g., "Free Shipping," "24/7 Support") — boosts credibility
  • Call Assets — Adds a phone number to mobile ads for direct calling — essential for service businesses
  • Location Assets — Shows business address; great for local businesses driving foot traffic
  • Structured Snippet Assets — Lists specific categories, products, or services under the ad
  • Lead Form Assets — Lets users submit contact info directly from the SERP without visiting the website — great for new advertiser lead gen
  • Image Assets — Adds visual images to Search ads
For new advertisers, I always recommend enabling as many relevant assets as possible — they increase ad real estate and provide more pathways to conversion.
10 What is Performance Max (PMax) and when should a new advertiser use it? CORE ▼
Model Answer
Performance Max is Google's fully automated campaign type that runs across all Google channels simultaneously — Search, Display, YouTube, Gmail, Discover, and Google Maps — using a single campaign structure. Google's AI decides where, when, and to whom to show ads based on your conversion goals.

Key features:
  • Requires conversion tracking to be set up (no PMax without it)
  • You provide "asset groups" — combinations of text, images, videos, and audience signals
  • More effective as it learns — typically takes 4–6 weeks to exit the learning phase
When it's a good fit for new advertisers:
  • They have a clear conversion goal (lead form, purchase)
  • They want maximum reach without managing multiple campaign types
  • They have strong creative assets (images, video)
When to be cautious: PMax offers limited visibility into where your budget is going. For very small budgets or brand-sensitive advertisers, starting with Search first gives more control before scaling into PMax.
PMax is a hot topic in Google Ads right now. Showing you understand its strengths AND limitations signals advanced knowledge.
11 What is the Google Keyword Planner and how do you use it? CORE ▼
Model Answer
Google Keyword Planner is a free tool within Google Ads used for keyword research and search volume forecasting. It helps:
  • Discover new keyword ideas related to a product or service
  • See monthly search volume ranges for specific keywords
  • Understand bid estimates (low and high range CPC)
  • Forecast expected impressions, clicks, and cost based on selected keywords and budget
How I use it during onboarding:
  1. Start with the client's business description or landing page URL to generate initial keyword ideas
  2. Filter by location (e.g., Toronto or Canada) and language
  3. Identify high-intent keywords (e.g., "buy", "hire", "near me") vs. broad informational terms
  4. Group keywords by theme for ad group structure
  5. Use the forecast tool to validate the budget against expected results
It's one of the first tools I'd use with a new advertiser to align expectations on what a realistic budget can achieve in their market.
12 What is the Google Display Network (GDN) and how does it differ from Search? CORE ▼
Model Answer
The Google Display Network (GDN) is a collection of over 2 million websites, apps, and videos where Google can place display ads. It reaches approximately 90% of global internet users.

Key differences vs. Search:
  • Intent: Search targets users who are actively looking for something (high intent). GDN reaches users who are browsing other content (lower intent, but wider audience).
  • Ad Format: Search = text ads. GDN = image, banner, responsive display ads, and video.
  • Targeting: GDN targets by audience (interests, demographics, custom intent, similar audiences) or by placement (specific websites/apps). Search targets by keywords.
  • Cost: GDN CPCs are typically much lower than Search. Good for cost-efficient awareness.
  • Goal: GDN is best for brand awareness, product discovery, and remarketing. Search is best for direct response and conversions.
For a new advertiser, I'd usually start with Search for immediate ROI and add Display for remarketing once we have website traffic to retarget.

13 How does Google Analytics 4 (GA4) connect with Google Ads, and why does this matter? CORE ▼
Model Answer
Google Analytics 4 (GA4) is Google's web analytics platform. When linked to Google Ads, it enables:

  • Richer Conversion Data — GA4 can pass conversion events back to Google Ads, so you can track micro-conversions (scroll depth, video views, engaged sessions) not just final sales
  • Audience Sharing — GA4 audiences (e.g., users who viewed a pricing page but didn't convert) can be imported into Google Ads for RLSA and Display remarketing
  • Full Funnel Visibility — See what users do after clicking an ad: pages visited, time on site, bounce rate — helping identify landing page issues
  • Smart Bidding Signals — GA4 data enriches Google's AI bidding with more behavioral signals, improving optimization
For new advertisers, linking GA4 to Google Ads is one of the first setup steps I'd walk them through. It's essentially the nervous system connecting advertising activity to real business outcomes.
Linking Google Ads + GA4 is almost always part of a new advertiser onboarding checklist at TP.
14 Explain how Google Merchant Center, Shopping Ads, and Google Ads work together for an e-commerce client. CORE ▼
Model Answer
For an e-commerce advertiser, the ecosystem works as follows:

1. Google Merchant Center (GMC) — The advertiser uploads their product catalog (name, price, image, availability, URL) into GMC as a product feed. Think of it as the "product database" for Google.

2. Google Ads — The Shopping campaign in Google Ads connects to the Merchant Center feed. When users search for relevant products, Google pulls product details from the feed to create Shopping Ads automatically — no manual ad copy writing needed.

3. Shopping Ads appear — At the top of search results with product image, price, store name, and ratings. These have higher purchase intent and typically strong conversion rates.

Synergy: Feed quality in GMC directly impacts Shopping Ad performance. A well-optimized feed (accurate titles, rich descriptions, competitive pricing) improves relevance matching and Ad Rank in Google Ads auctions. GA4 then tracks what happens post-click.

For a new e-commerce client, I'd help them set up GMC, verify their feed, link it to Google Ads, and run a Shopping or PMax campaign targeting their best-selling products first.
15 How does YouTube advertising complement a Google Search campaign for a B2B client? CORE ▼
Model Answer
YouTube and Search serve different stages of the buyer journey:

Search = Bottom of Funnel (BOFU) — The prospect is already aware of their problem and is actively searching for solutions. Search ads capture this high-intent moment.

YouTube = Top/Middle of Funnel (TOFU/MOFU) — YouTube is where you build brand awareness, educate prospects, and warm them up before they reach the search stage.

Synergy in practice:
  • A prospect sees a YouTube pre-roll ad explaining your B2B software solution and its ROI
  • Days later, they search "project management software for teams" on Google
  • Your Search ad appears — and because they've already seen your brand, CTR and conversion rate are higher
  • Using RLSA, you can bid more aggressively for users who watched your YouTube ad and then searched
Google Ads allows you to create Custom Intent Audiences and Video Reach Campaigns that target users based on their search behavior — bridging YouTube and Search for a full-funnel B2B strategy.
16 What is the role of Google My Business (Google Business Profile) in conjunction with Google Ads for local businesses? CORE ▼
Model Answer
Google Business Profile (GBP) — formerly Google My Business — is a free listing that shows your business on Google Maps and the Knowledge Panel in Search (name, address, hours, reviews, photos).

Connection to Google Ads:
  • Location Assets — Linking GBP to Google Ads enables Location Assets, which append your address and map pin to Search ads. This significantly increases ad size and visibility for local searches.
  • Local Campaigns / PMax for Store Goals — These campaign types use GBP data to drive in-store visits and calls.
  • Call Assets — Pulling your verified phone number through GBP adds credibility.
  • Maps Ads — A linked GBP allows ads to appear within Google Maps when users search nearby.
For a local business (restaurant, dental clinic, retail store), I'd always start by ensuring GBP is fully optimized and linked to their Google Ads account before launching any local Search campaign. The organic GBP listing also reinforces the paid ad, increasing overall visibility on the SERP.
17 How do you use Customer Match and audience targeting to enhance campaign performance? CORE ▼
Model Answer
Customer Match lets advertisers upload a list of customer emails (or phone numbers) to Google Ads. Google matches them to signed-in Google users and creates a targetable audience.

Use cases:
  • Win-back campaigns — Target lapsed customers with a special offer via Display or YouTube
  • Upsell/Cross-sell — Show current customers ads for complementary products
  • Exclusion — Exclude existing customers from acquisition campaigns to avoid wasted spend
  • Similar Audiences (Similar Segments) — Use the Customer Match list as a seed to find new users with similar profiles
Beyond Customer Match, audience targeting options include:
  • In-Market Audiences — Users actively researching in a category (e.g., "In-Market for Business Software")
  • Affinity Audiences — Broad interest categories
  • Custom Segments — Target users who searched specific terms on Google or visited specific websites
  • Life Events — Users going through major transitions (new job, moving, getting married)
For a new B2B advertiser, I'd combine In-Market + Custom Segments based on competitor URLs to reach the most relevant audience.
18 What is Google Tag Manager and why is it important in the Google Ads ecosystem? CORE ▼
Model Answer
Google Tag Manager (GTM) is a free tag management system that allows you to install, manage, and update tracking tags on a website without editing code directly.

Why it matters in Google Ads:
  • Deploy the Google Ads conversion tag and GA4 tracking code quickly without developer dependency
  • Set up event tracking (button clicks, form submissions, page scrolls) to feed conversion data into Google Ads
  • Manage the remarketing tag for building audience lists
  • Maintain clean, organized tag governance — reducing conflicts and page load issues
In a client onboarding workflow:
  1. Install GTM's container snippet on the client's website (one-time, minimal dev involvement)
  2. Deploy all tracking tags through GTM — Google Ads conversion, GA4, remarketing
  3. Test all tags with GTM's Preview mode before publishing
This setup makes future tag changes and optimizations much faster and reduces dependency on developers.
19 How does Smart Bidding use machine learning across the Google ecosystem? ADVANCED ▼
Model Answer
Smart Bidding is a subset of Google's automated bidding strategies that use machine learning to set bids at auction time, individually for every single search query. It pulls from a wide range of signals simultaneously:

Signals used in Smart Bidding:
  • Device, location, time of day, day of week
  • Browser and operating system
  • Search query and match type
  • Audience membership (remarketing lists, in-market segments)
  • Ad characteristics (format, extension configuration)
  • Landing page quality and historical conversion data from GA4
This is why the entire Google ecosystem working together — GA4, Merchant Center, GTM conversion tracking, GBP — feeds better signals into Smart Bidding and produces better results. A siloed Google Ads account with no linked properties is working with a fraction of the available data.

Key Smart Bidding strategies: Target CPA, Target ROAS, Maximize Conversions, Maximize Conversion Value. Each requires sufficient conversion history (typically 30–50/month) to function at full capacity.
Mentioning "auction-time bidding" and the breadth of signals shows you understand Smart Bidding at a deeper level than most candidates.
20 A client is running Search and Display but seeing poor overall results. How would you look at the full Google product stack to diagnose the issue? SCENARIO ▼
Model Answer
I'd work through a structured diagnostic across the full stack:

Step 1 — Conversion Tracking (GTM + Google Ads): Verify tags are firing correctly. Misconfigured conversion tracking is the most common root cause of "poor results" — the campaign may be performing fine but not reporting correctly.

Step 2 — Search Campaign: Review Search Terms Report for irrelevant traffic; audit Quality Score components; check if match types are too broad; review landing page alignment.

Step 3 — Display Campaign: Audit placement reports — often GDN wastes budget on mobile apps and parked domain sites. Add placement exclusions. Verify that audience targeting is meaningful and not "everyone."

Step 4 — GA4: Check post-click behavior. Even if clicks are coming in, if bounce rate is very high or time on site is under 10 seconds, the issue may be the landing page, not the ads.

Step 5 — Bid Strategy: Is the bidding strategy appropriate for the data volume? If there are under 30 conversions/month and they're on Target CPA, the algorithm is likely over-fitting or under-learning. Manual CPC or Maximize Clicks may be better temporarily.

Step 6 — GBP (if local): Confirm location assets are enabled and the GBP listing is accurate and well-reviewed.

I'd present findings to the client in a prioritized action plan, starting with the highest-impact fix.

21 A client tells you their goal is "more brand awareness." How do you translate that into a Google Ads campaign? SCENARIO ▼
Model Answer
Brand awareness is a broad goal, so I'd start by scoping the objective more precisely. I'd ask: Who is the target audience? What geography? What does success look like in 90 days — is it impressions, video views, branded search volume?

Translated into a campaign structure:

Primary: YouTube Video Campaign (Video Reach) — Use 15–30 second skippable in-stream ads targeting relevant In-Market and Affinity audiences. CPM bidding for efficient reach. Awareness at scale.

Secondary: Google Display Network (Smart Display or Demand Gen) — Broad awareness across millions of sites with responsive display ads. Use demographic and interest targeting.

Supporting: Branded Search Campaign — As awareness builds, branded search volume will increase. A branded Search campaign ensures you capture users who search your company name after seeing ads elsewhere.

Measurement: Track Impressions, Unique Reach, View Rate (YouTube), Brand Search lift (Search Impression Share on branded terms), and eventually traffic and conversion improvements compared to baseline.

I'd also set up a Brand Lift Study if budget allows, which Google provides for YouTube campaigns to measure actual lift in awareness and recall.
22 How do you structure a Google Ads account for a new advertiser starting from scratch? CORE ▼
Model Answer
A well-structured account follows this hierarchy: Account → Campaigns → Ad Groups → Ads → Keywords

My typical new account build:

Step 1 — Business Discovery: Understand their products/services, target customer, geography, budget, and primary conversion goal.

Step 2 — Keyword Research: Use Keyword Planner to identify intent-rich keywords; group them by theme.

Step 3 — Campaign Structure:
  • 1 campaign per major product/service category or business goal (e.g., "Plumbing Services Toronto," "HVAC Installation")
  • Ad groups within each campaign = sub-themes (e.g., "Emergency Plumbing," "Drain Cleaning")
  • 3–5 tightly themed keywords per ad group (avoid keyword stuffing)
  • 1–2 RSAs per ad group + Asset extensions enabled
Step 4 — Conversion Setup: Install Google Ads conversion tag via GTM; link GA4.

Step 5 — Bidding Strategy: Start with Maximize Clicks or Manual CPC while accumulating data; transition to Smart Bidding once 30+ conversions/month are established.

Step 6 — Negative Keywords: Build an initial negative keyword list to prevent obvious irrelevant traffic from day one.

Step 7 — Monitoring: Weekly Search Terms Report review, weekly performance check; optimize within the first 30 days based on real data.
Being able to walk through this end-to-end shows you can actually onboard a new client — this is exactly the role you're applying for.
23 A client has a monthly budget of $1,500. How would you allocate it across campaign types? SCENARIO ▼
Model Answer
With a $1,500/month budget, I'd prioritize based on proven ROI first, then layer in additional channels as performance data comes in. The exact split depends on their industry and goal, but a typical approach for a local service business:

$1,100 (~73%) — Search Campaigns
This is where high-intent, "ready to buy" traffic lives. For most new advertisers, this delivers the fastest ROI. I'd split across 1–2 core service campaigns with tight keyword targeting and strong negative keyword lists.

$300 (~20%) — Remarketing (Display or YouTube)
Retargeting past site visitors is highly cost-efficient. CPCs are low, and these users are already familiar with the brand. Even a small remarketing budget keeps the brand visible during the consideration phase.

$100 (~7%) — Buffer/Testing
Keep a small amount for A/B testing new ad variations, trying new ad assets, or experimenting with an additional ad group.

Key principle: I never set and forget a budget. I'd review performance weekly and reallocate toward what's converting. If Search is maxing out and performing well, I'd recommend increasing total budget rather than cutting it toward unproven channels.
24 What is a marketing funnel and how does Google Ads map to each stage? CORE ▼
Model Answer
The marketing funnel represents the buyer's journey from first awareness to final purchase:

🔝 TOFU — Awareness (Top of Funnel)
  • Goal: Introduce your brand to new audiences
  • Google Ads tools: YouTube (Video Reach Campaigns), Display (Affinity/Demographics), Demand Gen campaigns
  • Metrics: Impressions, Unique Reach, View Rate
➡ MOFU — Consideration (Middle of Funnel)
  • Goal: Educate prospects, build intent
  • Google Ads tools: Display (In-Market/Custom Intent), YouTube (Product/Solution-focused ads), Search (informational keywords)
  • Metrics: Clicks, Engagement Rate, Time on Site (via GA4)
🎯 BOFU — Conversion (Bottom of Funnel)
  • Goal: Drive purchase, lead, or sign-up
  • Google Ads tools: Search (high-intent keywords), Shopping, RLSA, PMax
  • Metrics: Conversions, CPA, ROAS, Revenue
🔄 Retention — Post-Conversion
  • Goal: Repeat purchase, upsell, loyalty
  • Google Ads tools: Customer Match remarketing, Gmail Ads, YouTube
For new advertisers with limited budgets, I always recommend starting at BOFU — capture existing demand first, then expand upward as ROI is proven.
25 How do you approach keyword strategy for a B2B vs. B2C advertiser differently? ADVANCED ▼
Model Answer
B2C:
  • Shorter sales cycle — keywords can be product-specific and transactional (e.g., "buy running shoes online")
  • Higher search volume on product-level terms
  • Price and promotions are strong CTAs in ad copy
  • Broad and phrase match can work well with Smart Bidding due to data volume
  • Shopping campaigns often outperform Search for product-based B2C businesses
B2B:
  • Longer, more complex sales cycles — keywords should map to multiple buying stages (awareness, evaluation, purchase)
  • Lower search volumes, higher CPCs, but higher lifetime value per conversion
  • Target role-based or problem-based terms: "fleet management software for enterprises," "reduce SaaS churn"
  • Prefer Exact and Phrase match for control — B2B budgets rarely allow for broad experimentation
  • RLSA and audience layering are critical — B2B sales involve multiple stakeholders and touchpoints
  • Lead form assets and call assets are high-priority for B2B conversion actions
Given my background in B2B tech sales, I approach B2B Google Ads like I would a consultative sales conversation — understanding the problem the prospect is trying to solve and mapping ad messaging to that pain point rather than leading with product features.
26 What is A/B testing in Google Ads and how do you run a meaningful ad experiment? CORE ▼
Model Answer
A/B testing in Google Ads means running two or more variations of an element simultaneously to determine which performs better, then scaling the winner.

What can be tested:
  • Ad copy headlines and descriptions (via RSA asset performance reports)
  • Landing pages (via Experiments tool in Google Ads)
  • Bid strategies (Manual CPC vs. Target CPA)
  • Campaign settings (e.g., broad match vs. phrase match)
  • Audience targeting overlays
How to run a meaningful experiment:
  1. Change only ONE variable at a time — otherwise you can't attribute the difference
  2. Run the test long enough to achieve statistical significance (typically 2–4 weeks minimum, or until you have ~100 conversions per variant)
  3. Use Google Ads Experiments tool for campaign-level splits — it divides traffic 50/50 by cookie, ensuring clean separation
  4. Define success metrics upfront: CTR, CPA, conversion rate
  5. Implement the winner and document learnings for future tests
In my sales role at Battle Infinity, I applied similar testing logic to outreach templates — iterating on subject lines and CTAs to improve response rates. The same systematic approach applies to digital ad optimization.
27 How do you write compelling Google Search ad copy for a first-time advertiser? CORE ▼
Model Answer
Strong Search ad copy follows a few principles:

1. Lead with the customer's goal, not the product: Instead of "We offer web hosting plans," write "Launch Your Website Today — Plans from $5/mo." Address the outcome the customer wants.

2. Include the keyword naturally: Users scan for relevance. Headlines that mirror their search query have higher expected CTR and improve Quality Score.

3. Create urgency or specificity: "Limited Time Offer," "Same-Day Service," specific numbers like "Save 30%" outperform vague claims.

4. Differentiate: What makes this business better than the 3–5 competitors showing ads on the same page? Surface it in the copy — "Rated #1 in Toronto," "30-Day Money-Back Guarantee."

5. Strong CTA: Tell users exactly what to do — "Get a Free Quote," "Book Online Now," "Start Free Trial."

6. Use all available character limits: Don't leave headline or description fields blank in RSAs. More assets = more combinations tested = better performance over time.

7. Match the landing page: The ad promise and the landing page headline should be nearly identical — any mismatch increases bounce rate and hurts Quality Score.
Practice writing 5 different headlines for a local plumber or a software company before your interview. It shows hands-on creative ability.
28 What is a negative keyword and give an example of a negative keyword strategy for a B2B software company. CORE ▼
Model Answer
Negative keywords prevent your ads from showing for irrelevant searches, protecting budget and improving campaign relevance.

For a B2B SaaS company (e.g., project management software for businesses):

Add negatives for:
  • "Free" — unless offering a free trial (blocks searchers seeking free forever solutions)
  • "Student," "school," "homework" — consumer/education audience, not B2B
  • "Jobs," "careers," "salary" — job seekers, not buyers
  • "Tutorial," "how to use," "review" — information seekers, not purchase-intent
  • Competitor names (unless running a conquest campaign intentionally)
  • "DIY," "template download," "open source" — budget-conscious non-buyers
Best practice:
  • Review the Search Terms Report weekly for the first 4 weeks after launch
  • Add irrelevant terms as negatives at the ad group or campaign level

45 What is Impression Share and how do you use it to identify growth opportunities? ADVANCED ▼
Model Answer
Impression Share (IS) is the percentage of total eligible impressions your ads actually received, compared to the maximum possible. It tells you how much of the available market you're capturing.

Formula: IS = Impressions Received ÷ Total Eligible Impressions

Two components tell you WHY you're losing IS:
  • IS Lost (Budget) — You're not showing because you've hit your daily budget cap. Solution: increase budget or tighten targeting to improve efficiency.
  • IS Lost (Rank) — You're not showing because your Ad Rank is too low vs. competitors. Solution: improve Quality Score (ad relevance, landing page) or increase bids.
How I use it strategically:
  • If a high-converting campaign has IS Lost (Budget) > 20%, that's a data-backed argument to the client for a budget increase: "You're missing roughly 1 in 5 potential customers due to budget constraints."
  • If IS Lost (Rank) is high, I focus on Quality Score improvements before recommending more spend
  • Competitor IS — Shows whether competitors are gaining or losing market share; useful context for client strategy conversations
Impression Share converts abstract "performance" data into a clear opportunity narrative that clients immediately understand.
46 How do you calculate and present ROI from Google Ads to a business owner? CORE ▼
Model Answer
ROI calculation:
ROI = (Revenue from Ads − Ad Spend) ÷ Ad Spend × 100%

Example: Spent $2,000. Generated 20 leads. 4 closed at $500 each = $2,000 revenue.
ROI = ($2,000 − $2,000) ÷ $2,000 = 0% — but that's only counting first-transaction revenue.

Better framing — Lifetime Value: If each client is worth $500/year and stays 3 years, the LTV is $1,500. Those 4 conversions represent $6,000 in LTV — 3x ROAS over lifetime, not just the first month.

How I present this to a client:
  • Always connect to their business economics: "What is a customer worth to you over 12 months?"
  • Use simple ratios: "For every $1 you spent, you got $3 back this month"
  • Show trend: Is CPA going up or down? Is ROAS improving?
  • Compare to alternatives: "Your CPA of $85 compares favorably to the industry average of $120 for this category"
Important caveat: Not all conversions are easily attributed to Google Ads. I'd always discuss attribution modeling (Last Click vs. Data-Driven) to make sure we're giving credit accurately without over-claiming.

48 What is attribution modeling in Google Ads and why does it matter? ADVANCED ▼
Model Answer
Attribution modeling determines how credit for a conversion is assigned across the multiple touchpoints a user had before converting.

Example: A user sees your YouTube ad on Monday, clicks a Display ad Wednesday, and then clicks a Search ad Friday before purchasing. Who gets credit for the sale?

Attribution models:
  • Last Click — 100% credit to the last ad clicked (Search, in the example). Default historically, but undervalues upper-funnel channels.
  • First Click — 100% credit to the first interaction (YouTube). Overvalues awareness touchpoints.
  • Linear — Equal credit distributed across all touchpoints.
  • Time Decay — More credit to touchpoints closer to conversion.
  • Data-Driven (DDA) — Google's machine learning assigns credit based on actual observed conversion patterns in the account. Most accurate but requires sufficient conversion volume (50+/month). This is now the Google-recommended default.
Why it matters: Attribution affects how Smart Bidding optimizes. A last-click model will under-fund YouTube and Display campaigns that assist conversions — hurting overall funnel efficiency. Data-Driven Attribution gives a more holistic view and allows smarter budget allocation across the full funnel.
